PROFESSIONAL ORIENTATION REFLECTION

WHY IS EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE IMPORTANT?

Emotional intelligence (the Emotional Quotient - EQ) came later than Intellectual Intelligence (measured as the Intelligence Quotient - IQ) as a concept but has been recognised as equally important. It is an important skill and set of attributes that helps someone to be successful in work and personal relationships. We are a social species and being with people, getting along, managing conflict, are necessary for wellbeing and good interpersonal relationships.

WHAT ARE EMOTIONS

Hasson describes emotions as made up of several parts: Physical sensations, thoughts, and behaviours. Hasson goes on to say that emotions are fleeting, differ in intensity, and are felt differently. For example, anger is a different experience to sadness. Emotions also differ according to the situation or context. Sadness might be felt in a situation of loss, or anger might be experienced in a situation where we are being attacked.

MANAGING EMOTIONS

Hasson says we need to manage emotions to improve Emotional Intelligence. However, more recent research shows that of the 3 characteristics of emotions, thoughts, sensations and behaviour, the thoughts and sensations of emotions are very difficult to control or change (Harris, 2021). We can change how we respond to emotions (behaviour) to make sure our behaviour is helpful and to make sure we don’t over or under react.

RESPONDING TO EMOTIONS

To respond helpfully to emotions, we first need to be aware, or notice, the emotion we are experiencing. We cannot manage our behaviour response to emotions in a situation without first realising what we are experiencing. This knowledge also prevents us from trying to avoid emotions, which as Hasson says, are part of being human. For example, anger is a normal emotion, but it is important that we know how to communicate it without causing harm to anyone, or anything else. If we try and avoid an emotion like anger, we may not be able to defend ourselves appropriately.

COMMUNICATION IS NOT THE SAME AS EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE

Hasson focusses on communication as if it is the same as EQ. However, some people are very good communicators but might not be very aware of their own emotions or anyone else’s (Harris, 2021) Some people can manipulate communication for their own reasons or to meet their own needs. Hasson does make important points about good respectful communication such as using “I” language, but communication is only a part of EQ.

IMPROVING EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE

Harris (Harris, 2021) states that knowing what is important to us (values) helps us with our emotional experiences because our values guide our behaviour. Mindfulness practice increases our self-awareness and so helps us to understand our emotions. Hasson gives the suggestion to do slow breathing if we are angry but without self-awareness, we can react to the anger without realising what is happening or be unable to express it at all.

THE PROBLEM WITH BODY LANGUAGE

Hasson emphasises communication too much for its role in emotional intelligence. Hasson says that understanding body language is important but does not describe how to learn this. Hasson does give the caution about being too confident about reading body language.

SUMMARY

Hasson has written about emotional intelligence and communication skills almost as if they are the same thing. Emotional Intelligence is a skill that can be learnt but is also influenced by many things like family background, personality, and experiences(Harris, 2021). In other words nature and nurture affect our Emotional Intelligence. Emotional Intelligence is our ability to understand our own and others emotions, and to learn ways to express these to improve our relationships with work colleagues, friends and family. It is a necessary part of healthy human relationships.
